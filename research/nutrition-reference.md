# Nutrition Reference — Tier 1 (Levels 1–4)

Reference facts for splash copy and level design. Each claim in the game should trace back to a line in this document. Flag anything that needs RD review with `[RD REVIEW]`.

## L1: Carbohydrates

### What They Are

- Carbohydrates are one of three macronutrients (along with protein and fat).
- The body breaks carbs down into glucose, its preferred and most readily available energy source.
- The brain relies almost exclusively on glucose for fuel (~120 g/day).

### Food Sources (Game Collectibles)

| Food | Notes |
|---|---|
| Bread (whole grain and white) | Both are carb sources; whole grain also provides fibre |
| Rice (brown and white) | Staple carb worldwide |
| Pasta | Quick-digesting carb source |
| Oats / porridge | Slower-digesting; also a fibre source |
| Potatoes (with and without skin) | Starchy carb; skin adds fibre |
| Fruit (banana, apple, orange, berries) | Contains natural sugars (fructose + glucose) plus fibre |
| Vegetables (sweetcorn, peas, carrots) | Starchier vegetables with meaningful carb content |
| Cereal | Common breakfast carb |
| Tortillas / wraps | Grain-based carb |
| Crackers / pretzels | Processed but still a carb source; not villainized |

### Starchy vs. Fibre-Rich Distinction (Level Challenge)

- **Starchy carbs:** Primarily provide glucose. Examples: white rice, white bread, pasta, potatoes (flesh).
- **Fibre-rich carbs:** Provide glucose AND fibre. Examples: fruits with skin, vegetables, oats, whole grains, potatoes with skin.
- This is a spectrum, not a binary. Many foods contain both starch and fibre. The gameplay distinction is about recognizing that some carb sources come with additional benefits (fibre), not that starchy carbs are lesser.

### Key Claim for Splash Text

> "Carbs are your body's preferred quick-energy fuel. They break down into glucose, which powers your muscles and your brain."

Source basis: Standard biochemistry — glucose is the primary substrate for glycolysis and the citric acid cycle. The brain consumes ~20% of the body's glucose supply (Mergenthaler et al., 2013).

## L2: Protein

### What It Is

- Protein is the body's primary structural macronutrient.
- Every tissue in the body is built from protein: muscle, skin, organs, hair, nails, bone matrix, tendons, ligaments.
- Beyond structure, proteins serve as enzymes (catalyze reactions), antibodies (immune defense), transport molecules (hemoglobin carries oxygen), and hormones (insulin).
- Protein is not the body's preferred energy source, but it *can* be used for energy via gluconeogenesis when carbs and fats are depleted. `[RD REVIEW]`

### Food Sources (Game Collectibles)

| Food | Complete/Incomplete | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Chicken breast | Complete | Contains all 9 essential amino acids |
| Eggs | Complete | Often called the "reference protein" for quality |
| Fish (salmon, tuna, cod) | Complete | Also provides omega-3 fats |
| Beef / lamb / pork | Complete | Animal-based complete protein |
| Tofu | Complete | Soy-based; one of few plant complete proteins |
| Lentils | Incomplete | Low in methionine; pair with grains |
| Beans (black, kidney, chickpeas) | Incomplete | Low in methionine; pair with grains |
| Nuts (almonds, peanuts) | Incomplete | Also provides fats |
| Yogurt / milk | Complete | Dairy-based |
| Quinoa | Complete | Grain-like seed; plant-based complete protein |

### Complete vs. Incomplete Proteins (Level Challenge — Combo Mechanic)

- **Complete proteins** contain all 9 essential amino acids the body cannot synthesize: histidine, isoleucine, leucine, lysine, methionine, phenylalanine, threonine, tryptophan, valine.
- **Incomplete proteins** are low in one or more essential amino acids.
- **Complementary pairing** — combining two incomplete proteins covers all 9:
  - Beans + rice (beans are low in methionine, rice is low in lysine — together they're complete)
  - Lentils + bread
  - Hummus (chickpeas) + pita
  - Peanut butter + whole wheat bread
- These pairings don't need to be eaten in the same meal — over the course of a day is sufficient. But for gameplay purposes, collecting them together triggers a combo bonus.

### Structural Roles (For Splash Accuracy)

| Tissue / System | Protein's Role |
|---|---|
| Muscle | Actin and myosin — contractile proteins |
| Skin | Collagen — structural integrity, elasticity |
| Hair and nails | Keratin — protective structural protein |
| Bone | Collagen matrix — gives bone its flexibility (minerals give hardness) |
| Immune system | Antibodies (immunoglobulins) — identify and neutralize pathogens |
| Digestion | Digestive enzymes (pepsin, trypsin) — break down food |
| Blood | Hemoglobin — transports oxygen; albumin — maintains blood pressure |
| Hormones | Insulin, growth hormone — signaling molecules |

### Key Claim for Splash Text

> "Protein is your body's building material. Every tissue you have — muscle, skin, organs, even your immune cells — is built and repaired with protein."

Source basis: Standard biochemistry. Protein synthesis and turnover account for ~20% of basal metabolic rate (Waterlow, 2006).

## L3: Fats

### What They Are

- Fats (lipids) are the most energy-dense macronutrient (~9 kcal/g vs. ~4 kcal/g for carbs and protein — use "energy-dense" in game, never "calorie" per guardrails).
- Fats are essential for: cell membrane structure, hormone production (steroid hormones, prostaglandins), absorption of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K), insulation and organ protection, and long-duration energy.
- Fat is a *slower* energy source than carbs — it takes longer to break down but provides sustained fuel.

### The Fat Spectrum

Fats exist on a spectrum, not in "good/bad" bins. The game presents them as a range:

| Type | Found In | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Monounsaturated** | Olive oil, avocado, almonds, peanuts | Generally supportive of heart health |
| **Polyunsaturated (Omega-3)** | Salmon, mackerel, sardines, flaxseed, walnuts, chia seeds | Anti-inflammatory; supports brain and heart |
| **Polyunsaturated (Omega-6)** | Sunflower oil, soybean oil, corn oil, sesame seeds | Needed but most diets already have plenty |
| **Saturated** | Butter, cheese, coconut oil, red meat fat, cream | The body uses these; the conversation is about proportion, not elimination |
| **Trans fats (artificial)** | Some margarines, commercially fried foods, some packaged baked goods | The only type broadly agreed to have no beneficial role; being phased out of food supply `[RD REVIEW]` |

### Key Claim for Splash Text

> "Fats are your body's slow-burn energy source. They also build every cell membrane in your body and help absorb essential vitamins."

Source basis: Fatty acids are integral components of phospholipid bilayers. Fat-soluble vitamin absorption requires dietary fat (Shils et al., Modern Nutrition in Health and Disease).

### Level Challenge — Variety, Not Avoidance

- The game mechanic rewards collecting a *variety* of fat types, not avoiding any single one.
- Collecting only one type results in diminishing energy returns.
- This teaches the nutritional concept that fat balance matters more than fat fear.

## L4: Fibre

### What It Is

- Dietary fibre is the portion of plant-based food that the body cannot fully digest or absorb.
- Unlike other carbohydrates, fibre passes through the stomach and small intestine relatively intact and reaches the large intestine (colon), where it feeds gut bacteria.
- Fibre is classified into two broad types:
  - **Soluble fibre:** Dissolves in water to form a gel. Slows digestion, helps regulate blood sugar and cholesterol. Found in oats, beans, lentils, apples, citrus fruits, barley.
  - **Insoluble fibre:** Does not dissolve. Adds bulk to stool and promotes regular bowel movements. Found in whole wheat, nuts, vegetables (cauliflower, green beans), potato skins.
- Most fibre-rich foods contain *both* types.

### Daily Targets `[RD REVIEW]`

| Group | Recommended Daily Fibre |
|---|---|
| Adult women | ~25 g/day |
| Adult men | ~30–38 g/day |
| Children (4–8) | ~18–20 g/day |
| Teens | ~22–31 g/day |

Source: Institute of Medicine Dietary Reference Intakes. Most adults consume only ~15 g/day — roughly half the recommendation.

### Food Sources (Game Collectibles)

|---|---|---|

| Black beans (½ cup) | Both | ~7.5 g |
| Lentils (½ cup) | Both | ~8 g |

| Broccoli (1 cup) | Insoluble | ~5 g |
| Whole wheat bread (1 slice) | Insoluble | ~2 g |

| Pear with skin (1 medium) | Both | ~5.5 g |
| Avocado (½ fruit) | Both | ~5 g |

### Gut Microbiome (Level Challenge — Microbiome Stations)

- The large intestine hosts ~38 trillion bacteria — roughly as many cells as the entire human body.
- These bacteria ferment soluble fibre, producing **short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs)** — particularly butyrate, propionate, and acetate.
- SCFAs fuel the cells lining the colon, support the gut barrier, modulate the immune system, and may influence mood via the gut-brain axis. `[RD REVIEW]`
- **Diversity matters.** Different fibre types feed different bacterial species. A varied diet = a diverse microbiome = a more resilient gut. This is the basis for the "microbiome diversity bonus" mechanic.
- The game's "microbiome stations" represent bacterial colonies. Delivering *different* fibre sources to each station (not just the same one repeatedly) powers them up more effectively.

### Key Claim for Splash Text

> "Fibre feeds the trillions of helpful bacteria in your gut. They keep your digestion strong, support your immune system, and even help your brain. Most people don't get enough — aim for variety!"

Source basis: Sonnenburg & Sonnenburg (2014), Gut microbiota and diet in health and disease. SCFAs' role in gut barrier function and immune modulation is well-established (Koh et al., 2016).

## Cross-Cutting Notes

### Language Guardrails Check

- No food in any of the four levels is labeled "bad." Trans fats are described as having "no known beneficial role," not as evil.
- Energy is always called "energy," never "calories."
- The word "healthy" is avoided in favor of specific functional language ("supports digestion," "provides sustained energy," "builds tissue").
- No body-shape or weight references anywhere.

### Overlap Between Levels

Some foods appear in multiple levels because they contain multiple macronutrients:
- **Nuts** — appear in L3 (fats) and L4 (fibre)
- **Beans/lentils** — appear in L2 (protein) and L4 (fibre)
- **Oats** — appear in L1 (carbs) and L4 (fibre)
- **Avocado** — appears in L3 (fats) and L4 (fibre)

This is intentional. In each level, the food is collected for its *relevant* macronutrient. The player gradually learns that real foods aren't single-nutrient — they overlap. This builds toward L14 (Balanced Plates).

### Items Flagged for RD Review

1. Protein used for energy via gluconeogenesis — phrasing needs to be precise about when this happens (chronic undereating, not routine)
2. Trans fat characterization — confirm current regulatory status and whether "no beneficial role" is accurate without qualification
3. Fibre daily targets — confirm age/sex breakdowns against most current DRI
4. SCFA and gut-brain axis claims — confirm level of scientific consensus for a general audience
